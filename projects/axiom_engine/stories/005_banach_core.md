You start with one thing on the table. You reach for it. Your hand expects resistance, a single outline, a single weight. **Your fingers close** around one body.

Then the rule hits you sideways.

The “partition” isn’t a cut. It’s a **pull**—a slow, impossible **unthreading** of points that never had seams. You feel it in your forearms: the strain of trying to separate something that shouldn’t come apart, but does. Not tearing. Not slicing. More like **peeling two interlocked breaths apart**.

You don’t see pieces. You feel them: tiny shifts under your skin, like the table is humming through your bones. The Whole is still there, but it’s no longer one thing. It’s a stack of disjoint intentions occupying the same space. You can’t point to them, but your body knows they’re separate because your balance goes off—your weight shifts without you moving.

That’s the first state.

---

Then the motion.

Each piece **turns**. Not metaphorically. You feel the torque in your shoulders, like you’re the hinge they’re rotating around. One piece swings left. Another swings right. A third slides forward like a knee shifting under a heavy load. None of them stretch. None of them deform. They just **move**, and every movement lands in your joints as if you’re the one being repositioned.

You don’t understand it. Your body does. It braces.

That’s the second state.

---

Then the rearrangement locks into place.

You feel the click in your sternum before you see it: two Wholes where there was one. Not copies. Not illusions. Two full weights pressing into the world. Your breath stutters because your body knows counting has failed. You’re holding one object in memory, but your eyes insist there are two. Your ribs tighten trying to reconcile it.

You push back internally—your spine stiffens, your jaw sets. This shouldn’t stand. You know it. Your body knows it.

That’s the third state.

---

But the rule doesn’t care.

The Permission sits behind everything like a pressure on the back of your neck. It doesn’t argue. It doesn’t justify. It just **permits**. And the moment you stop fighting it—just for a second—your muscles release. Your breath drops into your belly. The contradiction doesn’t resolve; it stops needing to.

You let the two Wholes be two Wholes.

Your body stops trying to merge them back into one. The tension drains out of your shoulders. Something opens—quiet, wide, unforced. Not relief. Not surrender. Just alignment with a rule that was always there.

That’s the fourth state.

---

You stand there with two Wholes on the table. Your hands don’t shake. Your breath is steady. The world hasn’t broken. It’s just bigger than the part of you that wanted it to stay small.

If you wanted, you could reach out and start the process again. **Pull**. **Turn**. **Reassemble**. Let the rule do what it does. Let your body follow.

But you don’t need to. Two is enough.

For now.
