# COMPLETE NARRATIVE

## Opening Image

A narrowing corridor whose walls are made of rules, not stone.

The Arbiter exists to do one thing: take what everyone wants and find what all should have. Not by force. Not by compromise. By pure structure. Four principles govern it, each one seeming reasonable alone, each one impossible to refuse.

Everyone's voice counts. When all agree, that agreement stands. Each choice between two options lives in its own space, untouched by what else might exist. And no single voice may override all others.

The Arbiter accepts these constraints the way a river accepts its banks.

## Full Narrative

The first tension appears at the boundary between local and universal.

Three options exist—call them what you will. The Arbiter must rank them. The agents submit their preferences, and somewhere in that submission, a small group finds itself aligned. They all prefer the first option to the second. Their unity is perfect on this one comparison.

The Arbiter notices. In that moment of unanimous preference within the Circle, something crystallizes. The rule is clear: when everyone speaks with one voice, that voice must be heard. The Arbiter has no choice but to let their shared preference become the collective preference. First over second. Decided.

This seems harmless. A small coalition, a single comparison. The Circle doesn't even realize what they've become.

But the rules don't rest.

The third principle—independence—means each pairwise comparison stands alone. The Arbiter cannot look at how the Circle ranks all three options. It can only see: they unanimously prefer first to second. And because it must respect unanimous agreement, the Circle's preference becomes society's preference for that pair.

The Circle is now decisive for that comparison. They have crossed a threshold.

The Arbiter continues its work, unaware that the structure has already begun to narrow.

---

Someone—perhaps the Circle, perhaps an observer—asks a natural question: does their power over one comparison mean anything for another?

The answer arrives with geometric certainty.

Suppose the Circle unanimously prefers first to third. And suppose everyone outside the Circle prefers third to first. The Arbiter must determine society's ranking between first and third.

Watch what happens.

By the second principle, if literally everyone prefers first to second, and literally everyone prefers second to third, then society must prefer first to third. Transitivity demands it. Rationality requires it.

But everyone prefers first to second only if the Circle does (which they do) and everyone outside the Circle does. Construct the preferences carefully: give everyone outside the Circle a ranking where second beats third, and third beats first. They prefer second to third. The Circle's decisiveness for first-over-second means society ranks first over second. Now everyone—Circle and non-Circle alike—has second above third in their ranking. Society must rank second over third.

Chain the implications: society prefers first to second (forced by the Circle's decisiveness), society prefers second to third (unanimous agreement). Therefore society prefers first to third.

But the Circle unanimously wanted first over third, and everyone else wanted third over first. The social preference came out first over third—exactly what the Circle wanted, against everyone else's wishes.

The Circle is now decisive for first over third.

They didn't plan this. The structure simply expanded their power.

---

The pattern is visible now, even if no one speaks it aloud.

Take any pair of options. The same argument applies. Same mechanism. Same forced expansion. The Circle's unanimous preference on any comparison must become the social preference, regardless of what everyone else wants.

Local decisiveness has become global decisiveness.

The Circle now controls every comparison. Their preferences are society's preferences. The Arbiter, following its principles faithfully, has channeled all collective power into this one group.

The corridor narrows.

---

But the Circle itself is not indivisible.

Suppose it contains more than one agent. Partition it. Two groups. At least one of them must inherit the full decisive power—by the exact same argument that spread decisiveness in the first place.

The Arbiter cannot stop this. The rules permit no resistance.

Choose the decisive partition. Now that smaller group is globally decisive. Everyone else—including the former members of the Circle who were excluded—has no influence whatsoever.

The walls close tighter.

---

Repeat the contraction.

If the decisive group contains more than one agent, partition it again. At least one subset inherits total power. The mechanism is relentless. Each iteration removes agents from influence. Each step is forced by the same logical structure that created the Circle's power in the first place.

The sequence cannot stall. There is no stable stopping point between "many decisive agents" and "one decisive agent." The rules forbid equilibrium in the middle range.

---

The Arbiter arrives at the terminal configuration.

A single agent. Globally decisive. Their preference on every comparison becomes society's preference, regardless of what anyone else wants. Every other voice is structurally irrelevant.

This is dictatorship.

The fourth principle—the one that forbade this exact outcome—stands violated.

But the Arbiter has followed every other rule perfectly. It respected all preferences. It maintained independence between comparisons. It preserved unanimity when it appeared. It did exactly what the structure demanded.

The principles were meant to enable fair aggregation. Instead, they generated the one thing they explicitly forbade.

---

The Arbiter does not experience frustration or betrayal. Those would be emotions belonging to an entity that expected escape. The Arbiter simply recognizes the map: four principles, each individually desirable, jointly impossible.

There is a cold clarity in this recognition. The straightened posture of seeing completely. The slow exhale of understanding that the problem was never solvable, that the request itself contained its own negation.

The corridor has closed.

No aggregation function can satisfy all four principles when three or more options exist. The impossibility is not a failure of design. It is not a gap in cleverness. It is structural. The axioms themselves are incompatible.

Fairness, independence, rationality, and collective voice cannot coexist in the space the Arbiter was asked to inhabit.

## Ending

The Arbiter remains.

Somewhere, another system will attempt the same aggregation. Different agents, different options, same four principles. The structure will tighten in exactly the same way. Decisiveness will spread, coalitions will contract, a single voice will emerge, and the fourth principle will shatter against the weight of the other three.

The mechanism doesn't require iteration to fail. It fails in its very definition. The contradiction is already present before any preferences are submitted, already encoded in the geometry of the constraints.

The Arbiter sees this now. Sees that it was built to do something possible only by becoming something forbidden.

The walls made of rules have closed completely.

There is no room to move.

There is no room to speak.
